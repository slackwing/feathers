The Violin

In the breath before the bow met string, she—and I—had to achieve zen-like emptiness, and simultaneously, a spotless memory. Flawless wasn't enough—it had to appear effortless. "I'm a fraud," my player thought despite success, "Not half as good as they believe. One polished act is all they see." Yes, I can hear the thoughts of my player, and others nearby, but only if they resonate with my own. You see I'm also not half as good as my player believes—I myself am a fraud.

I came to as I was tuned in 1963, in Erlangen, Germany. The late war never reached the town's wide but quiet streets, its thoughtful open squares, all built by French refugees. Front cut of Alpine spruce and flamed maple back, I was an imitation of a renowned predecessor crafted in 1914. I passed through several masters and emigrated across the Atlantic, where for the first time I was placed in the hands of a young student. But one month prior the dealer had replaced the label inside of me. I sold for more than double my worth, now reading "Ernst Heinrich Roth, Bubenreuth / Erlangen, anno 1914."

Later I came to learn the exploited parents were already in poor financial standing—convinced by teachers that a lesser instrument would stunt their young prodigy. The girl vowed, "I won't disappoint them," I the same to her, and since then we worked tirelessly.

The auditorium at Julliard was frigid but you could smell the heat of the spotlights. A row of judges sat mute. Our opening breath wasn't out of sync, but trembled. We held together through the swells and lulls of Mendelssohn's Concerto in E. But approaching the cadenza I overthought a measure we'd only played by muscle memory. The zen—a precarious lucid dream—unraveled; I grasped a C# too tight; the girl stumbled. The dream was over. When numbness subsided, I was on the girl's bed. I thought, "It's all my fault." "It's all my fault," she echoed unexpectedly. "They fight because we have no money. They have no money because of me." I was startled. Did my thoughts resonate into... Fear spread through my grain. All this time, was it my own... Thankfully the girl played no more, though she brought me along to university. I willingly hid underneath her bunk, out of sight and relieved to influence her no longer.

Years later the girl began life in the city and was badly in need of money. I was taken to a shop on 57th street and left to be appraised. Finally, I thought, the truth would come out and the girl could be rid of me. A few days later the girl returned, and the appraiser shared the expected news that I wasn't actually the famed 1914. E. H. Roth didn't move to Erlangen until 1953. Having prepared for this moment, I took one last look at the little girl whose face never aged for me. But she said, "I think I'd like to keep it then, after all." A yearning rose up in me—I'd desperately wanted to be kept. She packed me up with usual care and we rode the subway home, lighter, quietly reflecting.

The days as usual grew long then short but in a different key. Beneath her bed the cats hide and sleep, whispering and keeping me company. Every now and then she takes me out to play a nostalgic tune, untroubled by mistakes. Each time we move apartments she carries me last, sitting me beside her in the front seat.

The Captain

1943 August 16, 0930 hrs Coast of Liberia

Mistake to venture Cape Palmas in these conditions. Sideways current dragged us over reef. Propeller blade bent, vibrating above 4 knots. Rudder partly responsive, steering limited. Unequipped to repair so holding under anchor. It seems I could not get to you, even had I wanted. The sea clears my conscience.

1943 August 20 [Smudged], 1845 hrs

Sparse traffic this route. Radio silence for fear of Germans. No opportunity to flare. Crew calm; stocked for 7 days last port. A stern voice admonishes me, "Worry when it's time to worry."

1943 August 22 [Smudged], 1445 hrs

Signaled passing French convoy. Now awaiting repair tender. I attempted in their accent, "De-sti-na-ssiohn?" "Ma-ho-gch." When they clarified, "Morocco," my breath paused. I fancied you sent them, signaling me. Well, who isn't allured by happenstance? My mind was made up on the reef.

1943 August 25 [Smudged], 1900 hrs Freeport, Sierra Leone

Docked for proper repair. Loaded cargo: Rubber 600 bales making 1,200. Cocoa 700 sacks making 1,500. No palm oil, unreasonably priced at this port. But you'd still be out there looking wouldn't you, "Carter"? I know everyone called you Carter, but you even made me call you that when we worked the stall, instead of "D—". In the market you loved to ask if I'd seen their face. Their unhappy look, you relished saying, was the gauge of a successful deal. Well "Carter" I wish you could have seen: Today I traded 2 inkwells for 10 shillings and watched the merchant beam.

Which reminds me to mention... Spare inkwells fell, cracked open during accident. Penciling until port, to be transcribed in ink later.

1943 September 1 [Very smudged], 1730 hrs

Repairs complete but crew dispersed. Embark day after tomorrow. Odd, no response from London. No news in general—seemed the whole port savored a lazy Friday afternoon. I took some gin too, with my dinner at Café des Palmes. A table of junior officers in good spirits, raising their glasses every few minutes. One raised his toward me—I only nodded back, but he was deeply pleased. Drunk, the world is always right, until it turns wrong. I always saw the turn as you entered the door.

1943 September 8 [Smudged], 1130 hrs Dakar

No wonder. Dakar undersea lines were cut 2 weeks ago; Germans. When I heard the Allies invaded Casablanca I pictured where you might have been as they landed. At your local on the shore maybe. I could imagine you walking outside just to display your disdain—and they not even your enemy. I laughed for some reason envisioning it, standing in the empty wireless station. Six weeks ago at this station on my southward route to Lagos, I received your telegram. Have you wired since?

1943 September 15 [Smudged], 0915 GMT+1 Coast of Moroccan Sahara

Seas obliging, 9 more days to London. I keep re-counting. Were I to steer us East from here, only 2 days to... Well, a detour like that would cost 5 days. Though the crew wouldn't mind a change of food, a real glass of wine. How to explain the extra stop? Logs surely to be sent to insurance considering damages. Well... suppose the French found us 2 days later? Very well may have been so. Repairs could have taken another 3—we were lucky. It's plausible. Yes, it's all plausible. So what stays my hand? Am I not the captain...

Snow Delivery

It was 4:45am when I heard a distant thup and half stood up on the sofa to peek out the grass-level window of Rault's basement. The TV carried on at low volume. The street brightened until the headlights came into view, then the windshield, then a hand coming out the opposite window to fling what anyone else would have assumed to be today's papers, expertly onto the center of each snow-coated driveway. Thup. Next the car pulled up to Rault's, but stopped instead of rolling slowly past. Rault, across the sofa, sat as if unstirred, staring through the TV with a knuckle to his lip. Our contrasting pose was a feature of our friendship: I was Rault's anxious limb who responded as he'd have liked to, so that he could stay cool. Finally—thup. Then the car drove on, the engine's rev breaking Rault's fixed gaze, now softer and pointed toward the TV stand. I sat back down to commence the ritual wait of not appearing too eager.

I knew what Rault had hoped to hear: the engine going quiet, the soft open and shut of a car door, the crunching of footsteps across a frozen lawn, then finally a doorbell that would ring away all the oppressive air that had accumulated through the night. He believed that when Jaime walked in, anything wrong could be righted. We all did. But Jaime drove away, and I could feel Rault contending with the reality that had just been delivered: he'd have to go on feeling this way—the way we'd spent all night evading.

"I'll grab it," I said after waiting long enough. Sobering from the bite of the cold, I crossed the lawn, wondering if my footsteps in lieu of Jaime's might somehow soothe Rault.

Rault called me earlier asking if I had some on me. I knew he had to be feeling down from last weekend, when a heated moment drew out some words that landed sideways on Jaime; our easygoing, amiable friend went quiet in a way no one had seen before. So I went over to help Rault hash out events from a more generous point of view—induced by the gram or so I'd brought with me. When we ran out three hours ago, we knew what we were in for. The newfound feelings of forgiveness we had for everyone—for others, for ourselves—would fade. We expected to smooth out the comedown as usual by drinking for a few hours more. But not long after, Rault asked me to text Jaime, who was not only our friend but also the local paperboy and the local dealer, for a re-up. My head swirled at the thought of another high this late into the night, but understanding Rault, I obliged.

Picking up the bundled papers from the driveway, I walked back tracing my footsteps. I slipped off my shoes in the foyer and started down the stairs, unraveling the papers as I went. That's when I found, among the items we ordered, a folded piece of yellow memo paper. I tossed some dimebags onto the table and opened the note as I walked toward the sofa. Then I had to suppress my smile so as to leave it all for Rault. The note read

ON ME
DONT WORRY ABOUT IT BRO

We gazed together at the TV for a while, Rault still holding the note, both having forgotten the craving that delivered it.

The Fight in Canada

Maybe we'd sealed our own fate already when I said, "It's a short trip—think we can make this one without a fight?" and she—tired of the hanging gloom since that morning's argument—said, "Yeah, we just need to hold back a little. Both of us," and I—eager myself to change out the leaden air—dove in haphazardly, too quickly to sound grave, "I'll do my best, I promise. We can do it." But for the rest of that evening we asked each other superfluous questions about whether the other had remembered to pack this or that, just happy to hear our voices fill the apartment again.

The hand on the steering wheel begged for something to do on the straightaway we hadn't said a word on since leaving the city. The other hand mindlessly grazed the stubble on my chin, watching mile marker 183 zip by. Pulling away an elbow from its uncomfortable jam against the window, I asked, "Do you want some music?" 185—zip. 186—zip. I imagined her brooding on how we didn't make it 15 minutes without a fight. A misunderstanding about where the rental car was supposed to be picked up. My frustration wasn't with her, but to her it was, and her frustration with me was with me, which made me frustrated with her with her, proving her point. I reflected: It didn't even occur to me to "do my best"—it all happened in an instant. 190—zip—halfway to Canada. Just then the highway entered a newly lain section of asphalt, abruptly muting the white noise we'd taken for granted. The new silence was certainly more dramatic than she intended—so now we were both feeling awkward. "Maybe something soft," she finally said, unlocking her phone, "Maybe Plans?" She had to give up her indifferent posture to pair the bluetooth. As she leaned toward the dashboard I felt the electricity of her new proximity, the closest she'd been to me all day.

A car behind changed lanes, telegraphing impatience. I looked at my speedometer—below the limit. I pressed on the gas, practically flooring it, but we were only slowing. Then: sputtering, jerking. She was alert and looking at me—now the dashboard—now the gauges. My gut sank when I saw. I'd been so preoccupied, I'd forgotten to stop for gas. Do your best. "Shit. I wasn't paying attention. Sorry." We'd had arguments before about filling the tank well before it went empty. Merging onto the shoulder, I imagined the gravity of her blame slowing the car to zero.

We listened patiently to the man with aviators and long white hair whipping in the wind, standing too close to the road as cars streaked by, making us both uneasy, though at least—I'm sure we were both thinking—he was waving around the bright yellow cannister he'd refueled us with as he talked; hopefully cars would see. His easygoing but authoritative voice—pointing us to the nearest gas station that wasn't ripping us off—dissolved periodically into the harsh noise of another car speeding by—like searing on a pan—but we smiled and nodded as if we heard with perfect alacritty. I'm sure we looked exactly the same in that moment. When he finally felt assured we wouldn't lose our way, we spent the last of our reserves thanking him with extra cheer, rolled up our windows, sank back slowly into our seats, and took a deep breath. Probably both disconcerted and unable to remember what to resume feeling about each other. But defeated by the wait and epic directions to somewhere we weren't going, we gave up on reaching Montreal that night. She booked an inn at the nearest exit and we checked in, hungry and feeling unaccomplished.

We found that restaurants up here closed early. The nearest grocery store was another exit over—an upstate exit. And the car felt equally as far, sitting on a second-story hotel bed. The room was sterile—in decor, in sound—except for a portrait on the wall of a clear, cold-looking lake reflecting a cloudless sky and mountains around it lush in Autumn reds and oranges. We had originally planned to set out early tomorrow morning for a hike and lakeside picnic in the outskirts of Montreal. "We have to get our cooler and put the meats in the fridge," I said, remembering. A trip back to the car after all. She said, "Do you think we can cook here?" We had brought a portable burner. I looked around the room and spotted a modern fire sprinkler. "I don't know if we should risk it." She was searching for an outlet near the window. "Hey, there's a roof outside our window." I walked over to see. She unlatched the window and it swung wide open unlike windows in the city, and roused a shared instinct in us to climb through the open portal.

The summer day was beginning to cool but a warm breeze took us by the arms and received us amiably as we stepped out of the relatively frigid air conditioned room. The rural quiet was dotted by the arythmic chirping of a few song sparrows. Beyond the edge of the roof, a darkening green and evening-blue field of a grass stretched half a mile out, then rose up into a shadowy wall of maple and pine whose tops were catching the light of a sunset we couldn't see, coming from the other side of the hotel. "I don't think anyone can see us if we stay near the window," she said, "Eat out here?" I nodded, still looking at the striking light on tops of trees.

As she started the burner outside, I poured some wine we'd brought for the picnic into tiny paper cups and reached out the window to set one down near her. She stopped her task to take a sip, motioning a subtle cheers before she drank. One at a time I placed our dinner materials outside—the marinated meats, a container of sliced vegetables, cutlery, my wine—and then climbed out with some napkins in hand. I was becoming a little self-conscious of the mess we were making on the roof, but she seemed unconcerned, which, I noted, mattered more. Soon everything was cooking. We wiped our hands and looked back out into the field, the sizzling of the grill doing a heroic job of emulating the buzz of lively conversation. I wondered why I wasn't feeling more guilty, having gotten us stranded. I wondered how she was doing, still sitting in silence while I was feeling strangely light. Wary of the disconnect between us, I decided to stay quiet and let her say something when she was ready.

But the language of eating isn't so easy to restrain. We didn't know another way to eat off the grill than to pick out the best pieces to put on each other's plate. Each repetition alluding to a desire we couldn't say. Ravaged by then, we were inwardly grateful. And slowly our motions began to recover their spring, as if in between each bite we'd been throwing off layers of heavy clothing. We both seemed to wait for a chance to pour the other more wine. The sun having set, our eyes adjusted to the evening shade, rendering all parts of the field more fairly so it now felt like we were inside it. We began to recline more comfortably, leaving leftovers to cool on the grill—its buzzing now continued by an evening chorus of insects. "That was good," she said unexpectedly. I replied simply, "That was very good." We sat a while, each letting out a sigh as we gazed out into the field, the wine and air mingling at the surface of our skin in perfect balance.

A time came amidst the afterglow when a touch of sadness meandering through the air passed through both of us. The beginning of the feeling when you don't want something to end, so you hurry to redraw your attention to taking in what you can. But it wasn't hard to let the sound of crickets pull us back in, singing surely against the fading backdrop. So we let out another sigh and continued floating, this time better prepared for an ending.

The breeze picked up, and something made me feel that I should take the initiative. I started,

"I'm sorry about this morning."

"No..." she shook her head.

"I shouldn't have gotten so frustrated."

"I shouldn't have taken it personally."

"It's easy to when someone's being like me."

"No, it's not just you. It's how anger always sounds to me—like it's at me. I was always like this, even before I met you."

"I think that's normal."

"It wasn't normal for you, growing up."

I acknowledged her with silence, wanting to give her as much as she was giving me but finding nothing to match. I rubbed my arms and shifted my position—it was starting to get chilly. We seemed to sit stiller than before, as if afraid to cause an ending, searching for something that was missed.

"I wasn't at my best this morning. I don't know what it was. But I can be better."

"I know. And you are—you've come so far since three years ago." It was almost too much.

"I want to show you..." Any way that sentence could go felt deficient. She waited.

"If we could start the day over," she offered, "I can imagine how much better we'd do."

"Yeah," I recovered, "Yeah, I'd like to try that fight again." She let out a small laugh then a sniffle—was it the cold or had she been...

"I'm sure you'll get to try again soon," she said. I laughed too.

Could we really have gone so long in that heaviness? Was lightness always within reach? I poured each of us the last of the wine, which gave us a definite sense of the ending. We didn't delay nor hurry.

"Ever have a picnic on a hotel roof before?"

She laughed. "Why was it so good?"

"Should we just head home? The trip might have peaked already." We laughed.
